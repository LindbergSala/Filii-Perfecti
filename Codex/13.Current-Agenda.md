In the present age, the Filii Perfecti do not pursue conquest in the manner of empire-builders, nor do they labour towards some vast and coherent dominion such as might preoccupy the older powers of the Long War. Their ambitions are at once narrower and more poisonous. They seek not territory for its own sake, nor simple military renown, but the continued refinement of appetite into hierarchy, and hierarchy into apotheosis. Every campaign, raid and ritual undertaken by the warband is ultimately bent towards one of three ends: the acquisition of worthier blood, the elevation of favoured warriors within the courts of the Equites Vindemiae Rubri Graalis, and the ever-receding hope that one amongst them might achieve the ultimate vindication of their creed through ascension into daemonhood.

At the centre of these present ambitions stands Dominus Valerius Cruentus and his obsessive pursuit of the Cruor Perfectum. The warlord’s hunger has ceased to be a private vice and become the orienting principle of the warband’s wider movement. He seeks a vitae so pure, so noble in lineage, and so exquisitely saturated with conscious suffering that its consumption would suspend him forever at the threshold between thirst and fulfilment. In his own blasphemous conception, such a draught would not merely intoxicate or empower. It would crystallise the truth of the sons of Sanguinius into its final form, proving beyond all contradiction that the curse was always a sacrament awaiting perfection. Thus many of the warband’s current predations are directed less by strategic map-logic than by the scent of possibility: rumours of wounded scions of Baal’s line, isolated reliquary worlds, shattered crusade fronts rich in noble dead, and shrines where blood and memory lie close enough together to be harvested as one.

For the broader warband, this makes their agenda simultaneously coherent and unstable. Coherent, because nearly every aspirant, knight and artisan within the Filii Perfecti understands advancement through the same symbolic vocabulary. To drink better blood, to orchestrate more exquisite suffering, to rise through duel, patronage and revealed appetite, and ultimately to win the eye of Slaanesh — these are aims shared across the court, however viciously pursued. Yet it is also unstable, because those aims do not naturally produce unified strategy. The Perfect Sons are not a host marching towards a single fortress or a legion bent upon a clearly defined sector war. They are a moving aristocracy of rival hungers, kept aligned only so long as the promise of richer sensation lies in roughly the same direction for all.

This is why their recent movements have increasingly drawn them into the wake of greater Slaaneshi disturbances. The warband is acutely aware that the current age is one of widening revelation for the servants of the Dark Prince. The upheavals associated with the return of daemon primarchs, the fracturing of old frontiers, and the ever more brazen manifestations of the warp have created a climate in which acts of exceptional devotion may be more visibly rewarded than in lesser centuries. The Filii Perfecti are not so arrogant as to believe themselves central to such epochal events, yet they are more than eager to position themselves near their edges. Their participation in conflicts surrounding the wider Slaaneshi agitation, including those currents of violence later associated with Fulgrim: The Perfect Son, reflects this instinct. They do not imagine themselves indispensable to the grand design of Fulgrim or the Emperor’s Children. Rather, they wish to be present where significance gathers, to drink from the overflow of greater blasphemies, and to prove that though they are not of Fulgrim’s blood, they are nonetheless worthy cousins in excess.

Their present objectives therefore operate on two intertwined levels. The first is immediate and practical: secure blood, captives, gene-stock and sacramental opportunities sufficient to sustain the warband’s hierarchy and ongoing self-remaking. The second is aspirational and theological: convert those resources into moments of transcendence so potent that rank, doctrine and appetite all climb another notch towards daemonhood. In this, every major figure within the warband reads the future slightly differently. Valerius seeks perfection concentrated in a single ultimate vintage. Severian Albus seeks a battlefield order in which every challenge becomes a ladder rung towards princely primacy. Octavius Carnifex seeks continuity through deeper command of blood and implantation, ensuring the warband endures long enough for any dream of apotheosis to matter. Armand Callix seeks the perfect union of rite and slaughter, a liturgical consummation in which the warband’s every deed becomes one sustained hymn to Slaanesh. Lysander Thyr Vhal, more dangerously, appears to seek a future in which the old elegance of the warband is broken open, allowing more radical transformations to claim the centre of its creed.

In purely strategic terms, the realism of these ambitions is questionable. The warband can certainly continue to raid, harvest and endure. It has already proven that much. It can wound worlds, despoil shrines, corrupt captives and preserve itself through a hideous economy of blood. It may even elevate individual lords to ever more terrible heights of favour. But the greater hopes that animate the Filii Perfecti — a permanent arrest of the thirst at its most ecstatic edge, the distillation of a truly perfect blood, the seamless conversion of inherited curse into divine ascent — bear all the hallmarks of Slaaneshi promise: just near enough to seem attainable, just distant enough to demand endless escalation. In this sense, their agenda is both realistic and delusional at once. Realistic in the short term, because it produces action, prey and temporary victories. Delusional in the ultimate sense, because the appetite it serves can never truly be completed without destroying the self that chases it.

Were the warband to define success in the present era, it would likely look less like territorial dominion and more like a chain of converging consummations. A triumphant campaign for the Filii Perfecti would see worthy sons of Sanguinius captured in number, their bloodlines sorted and rendered into new sacramental hierarchies. It would see rivals within the Sodalitas Cruoris either elevated or broken, leaving the inner court more potent and more refined. It would see one or more of the warband’s greatest lords step visibly closer to daemonic reward, perhaps through mutation so perfect it reads as revelation rather than decay. Above all, success would mean that the warband had tasted something it had never tasted before: a higher vintage, a nobler agony, a sharper proof that the path of the Perfect Sons is not merely corruption, but destiny.

Yet the cost of such success is written plainly enough for any cold observer. To move nearer their dream, the warband must intensify every practice that already threatens its cohesion. It must hunt the sons of Sanguinius ever more aggressively, drawing the wrath of Blood Angels successors and Imperial hunters alike. It must spend precious gene-stock, risk its blood-vaults and expose its fleets in pursuit of increasingly selective prey. It must indulge the rivalries of the Equites, because without such rivalry there is no ascent — but with too much of it there is only civil predation. It must court more visible favour from Slaanesh, knowing that every blessing carries also the erosion of recognisable selfhood. In short, to succeed as they presently define success, the Filii Perfecti must become more fully what they already are, and in doing so risk ceasing to remain coherent enough to enjoy the victory.

This is the present tragedy of the Filii Perfecti. They are not aimless. Quite the opposite. They are driven by an agenda of terrible clarity. Every chalice they raise, every duel they prolong, every captive they preserve for exquisite unmaking, and every raid they launch into the blood-soaked dark of M42 serves the same higher hunger. But the object of that hunger recedes as they approach it. The closer they move towards perfection, the more exacting perfection becomes. The nearer they draw to apotheosis, the more it demands of them in blood, form, loyalty and restraint surrendered. They do not yet recognise this as failure. In their own eyes, it is simply proof that there are still greater heights to climb.

Thus the Filii Perfecti drift onward through the broken galaxy: not empire-makers, but seekers of the next impossible draught. Their present agenda is not to rule the stars, but to find within them a blood fine enough, a pain pure enough, and a revelation strong enough to justify the name they still dare to bear.
