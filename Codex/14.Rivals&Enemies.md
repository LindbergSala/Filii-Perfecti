The Filii Perfecti cultivate hatred with the same care they apply to blood, ritual and appearance. They do not regard enmity as a crude by-product of war, but as a necessary intensifier of experience. A hated foe bleeds more sweetly. A rival’s humiliation carries more flavour than the slaughter of the nameless. Thus the warband’s most enduring enemies are not merely those who threaten its survival, but those whose existence wounds its creed, contradicts its self-image or mirrors some truth it would prefer to conquer rather than confront. The Perfect Sons are, in this as in all things, selective in their malice.

Foremost amongst their Imperial hate-objects stand the surviving sons of Sanguinius. No other foes so consistently draw the warband’s obsession, nor provoke in them such a mixture of hunger, contempt and sacrilegious longing. To the Filii Perfecti, the loyal bloodline of the Great Angel embodies the central lie against which their entire creed is constructed. Their former kindred still resist the Red Thirst, still mourn the Black Rage, still frame their suffering as burden rather than revelation. This, in the eyes of the warband, is not nobility. It is denial. Every Blood Angel or successor who refuses the “truth” embraced by the Perfect Sons is therefore judged a living blasphemy against what Sanguinius’ legacy should have become. Such enemies are never merely targets. They are arguments to be disproven in blood.

Within that broader hatred, the Blood Angels themselves retain a uniquely charged place. The memory of Dante’s judgement, the erasure of the Chapter’s honour, and the symbolic weight of Baal’s surviving authority ensure that the sons of the Great Angel are more than convenient prey. They are witnesses who refused absolution. Every raid against a Blood Angels successor shrine, every capture of a Sanguinary Priest, every desecration of blood-relics is in part an attempt to answer that original condemnation. In the warband’s perverse theology, Dante did not cast them out for falling. He cast them out for daring to interpret the blood more honestly than he could bear. Whether any amongst the Filii Perfecti truly believe this is immaterial. They act as though it were true, and that makes their wars against the lineage of Baal especially intimate and especially vicious.

Where possible, the Perfect Sons prefer not to exterminate such enemies outright. Extinction is too blunt. They would rather capture, corrupt, prolong and display. A slain successor captain may become a ledger-entry or chalice-mark. A captured apothecary or Sanguinary Priest may become something infinitely more valuable: proof that the sacred can be made to sing under the knife. Thus their wars with the bloodline of Sanguinius tend to generate relic-thefts, disappearances, opened sanctums and massacres staged with terrible symbolic literacy. To Imperial observers this gives their campaigns a particularly hateful character. They do not simply kill sons of Baal. They unmake what those sons revere, and do so with the intimacy of former kin.

Beyond the bloodline of Sanguinius, the warband has also earned the enmity of more particular Imperial agents. Fragmentary reports attribute several coordinated hunts against the Filii Perfecti to elements of the Ordo Hereticus and the Ordo Astartes, especially after the wider implications of The Vintage of the Red Grail became partially understood. Inquisitors who specialise in gene-line corruption, shrine desecration and post-Rift renegade formations have found in the Perfect Sons a uniquely intolerable adversary: a recent-fall warband whose crimes are not only doctrinally monstrous, but symbolically contagious. The fear is not merely that the Filii Perfecti kill, but that their existence itself invites dangerous thought. If a newly forged Primaris Chapter could descend into such elegant blasphemy, what other restorations might yet conceal rot beneath polish? For that reason, some Imperial hunts against them have taken on the character of eradication campaigns rather than standard pursuit. The warband, unsurprisingly, interprets such zeal as confirmation of its significance.

If Imperial enemies represent shame externalised, then the warband’s greatest Chaos rivals represent competition before the gaze of Slaanesh. In theory, the Filii Perfecti are brethren to all who serve the Dark Prince. In practice, they are connoisseurs in a court too crowded with aspirants. Every Slaaneshi warband, cult host, daemon court or wandering apostolic congregation is a potential rival for favour, spectacle and sacramental prestige. The Perfect Sons rarely descend to indiscriminate hatred of these kindred, for they recognise shared appetites and may at times fight beside them. Yet beneath any temporary alliance lies a taut and poisonous truth: every rival servant of Slaanesh is another mouth raised towards the same cup. There is only so much notice a god may seem to bestow in any given hour, and those who live for notice soon learn to resent one another with exquisite fervour.

Only the Emperor’s Children are granted a more complicated status. The Filii Perfecti do not view Fulgrim’s Legion as inferiors or vulgar imitators, even when they recoil from some of its more openly riotous excesses. Rather, they regard them as terrible and respected cousins, ancient exemplars of the path the Perfect Sons themselves now walk by a different route. They are not trusted, and certainly not loved, but neither are they merely rivals in the same sense as lesser Slaaneshi hosts. The warband knows that to stand before the children of Fulgrim is to face a lineage older in corruption and more direct in its relationship to the Dark Prince. This creates in the Filii Perfecti a deeply unstable mixture of admiration, envy and defensive self-assertion. They wish to be seen as distinct, not derivative. They wish to prove that though they are not of Fulgrim’s blood, they have discovered something the Emperor’s Children did not: a way to fuse the angelic curse of Sanguinius with Slaaneshi ecstasy into a creed neither Legion can wholly claim as its own.

This unstable respect becomes especially fraught when the warband moves in the orbit of wider Slaaneshi events. During the disturbances linked to Fulgrim: The Perfect Son, for instance, the Filii Perfecti were not central architects of the conflict, but they were drawn to it precisely because such moments gather both opportunity and comparison. To appear in such a theatre is to risk being dismissed as minor parasites feeding on a greater revelation. It is also to gain the chance to prove, through deed and atrocity, that one’s own flavour of devotion is worthy of notice. Thus the Emperor’s Children function for the Perfect Sons not as clean enemies, but as an aristocratic mirror before which insecurity, aspiration and rivalry become nearly indistinguishable.

There are also more pragmatic Chaos enemies. Warbands devoted to Khorne are often held in particular disdain, not solely because of battlefield incompatibility, but because they embody the exact form of bloodshed the Filii Perfecti reject: crude, explosive, undiscerning violence that destroys texture beneath frenzy. To a Khornate berserker, blood is tribute through volume. To the Perfect Sons, blood without refinement is mere waste. Conflicts between the two are therefore often ideologically charged even when strategically accidental. Likewise, some hosts of Nurgle disgust the warband on aesthetic grounds, for where the Perfect Sons cultivate immaculate surfaces and elegant torment, the plague-ridden revel in rot, slackness and the collapse of distinction beneath decay. In both cases, the difference is not merely doctrinal. It is sensory. The warband truly experiences such foes as offensive to the standards by which it lives.

Yet the most dangerous enemy of the Filii Perfecti is neither Imperial hunter nor rival warband. It is the warband itself. Beneath every external conflict lies a more intimate fault-line: the fact that the Perfect Sons are united by the very appetites most likely to tear them apart. Their hierarchy offers elevation, but never enough. Their blood economy creates loyalty, but only through deprivation. Their rituals bind brother to brother, but always under the eyes of patrons, rivals and aspirants calculating how suffering may yet be converted into greater rank. This makes their internal enmity not an aberration, but a permanent atmospheric condition. Every gathered feast is also a potential court intrigue. Every duel of honour contains a future vendetta. Every gift of blood creates debt, and every debt breeds resentment.

At the highest level, this internal hostility is increasingly personified by the tension between Dominus Valerius Cruentus and those figures who may one day contest not only his supremacy, but the very shape of the warband’s future. Severian Albus represents the threat of martial vanity sharpened into princely ambition, a warrior who may one day decide that elegance itself entitles him to rule. Lysander Thyr Vhal embodies a subtler but perhaps more destabilising danger, gathering influence amongst those who hunger for more radical flesh-transformation and less restraint in the acceptance of Slaanesh’s mutational generosity. Octavius Carnifex, for all his outward loyalty, controls the very blood and gene-seed infrastructures that keep the warband viable, and so remains dangerous simply by being indispensable. Even Armand Callix, symbol of the warband’s soul, may become perilous should liturgical purity come to outweigh pragmatic necessity. Thus the inner court is less a stable command tier than a chandelier of knives, each blade glittering beautifully until it turns.

This internal rivalry is not hidden from the wider warband. On the contrary, it is institutionalised through patronage, the Duellum Agoniae, access to blood reserves and the graded favour of the Equites Vindemiae Rubri Graalis. Such mechanisms delay collapse by giving enmity a form, but they do not remove it. Indeed, they intensify it by promising legitimacy to hatred. A slight may become a formal challenge. A grievance may become a sanctioned humiliation. A disappointment may become exile to the Cellulae Sitis Perditae. Even brotherhood is sharpened against itself, for the Perfect Sons can scarcely imagine a relationship that is not also a ladder, a threat or an audience.

What happens, then, when the warband encounters its deepest hate-object in full? Imperial after-action testimonies provide a grimly consistent answer. If the Filii Perfecti are brought into direct conflict with worthy sons of Sanguinius — especially those of noble status, sacred office or visible symbolic importance — their warfare becomes even more intimate, more theatrical and more dangerous. They will abandon efficiency for ritual. They will prolong the battle to secure living captives. They will open reliquaries in view of defenders, force duels that should have been executions, and risk overextension merely to ensure that the enemy dies as argument rather than obstacle. This makes them terrible to face, but also vulnerable. An opponent who understands that the Perfect Sons crave symbolic consummation may use that craving against them, baiting them into exposure or delay.

The same principle holds for rival Slaaneshi hosts. When another servant of the Dark Prince threatens to eclipse them — whether by spectacle, mutation, proximity to a daemon prince or simple theatrical dominance — the warband responds not with measured coalition-building, but with escalating acts of distinction. They seek to outdo, out-curate and out-suffer their rivals. Such contests may begin as alliance and end as massacre. In a universe ruled by predatory gods, this is hardly unusual. Yet among the Filii Perfecti it is raised almost to art. To be eclipsed is intolerable. To share the stage is barely better.

Thus the enemies and rivals of the Filii Perfecti are not a simple list of military opponents, but a map of wounds. The sons of Sanguinius remind them of what they were and what they profaned. The Emperor’s Children remind them that they are not the only aristocrats in the court of Slaanesh. Other Chaos powers offend their cultivated senses. Their own inner hierarchy ensures that every brother may one day become antagonist. In all this there is a strange coherence. The Perfect Sons require enemies because enemies provide contrast, and contrast sharpens sensation. Without resistance, there is no exquisite victory. Without rivals, no meaningful ascent. Without hate, even blood may begin to taste thin.
